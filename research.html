Research

We study how developmental signaling programs build and maintain synovial joints—and how disruptions in these programs contribute to osteoarthritis.

Overview

From development to lifelong tissue health

Synovial joints must form correctly during development, mature after birth, and remain durable under decades of mechanical load. Our lab investigates how spatial and temporal signaling cues pattern joint tissues (cartilage, synovium, and associated structures) and how altered signaling predisposes joints to degeneration.

  • Development: how joints and their tissues are specified and patterned.
  • Postnatal maturation: how articular cartilage architecture and zonation emerge.
  • Disease: how dysregulated pathways drive osteoarthritis onset and progression.
Research themes

What we work on

Synovial joint development

How joint boundaries and synovial tissues are patterned, and how early developmental decisions influence tissue organization later in life.

Articular cartilage morphogenesis

Mechanisms that build postnatal cartilage architecture, including zonal organization and transitions that support long-term function.

Osteoarthritis pathogenesis

How disrupted signaling and tissue architecture contribute to degeneration, with the goal of identifying actionable nodes for prevention or repair.

Approaches

How we do it

Genetics & mouse models

Tissue- and time-specific perturbations to test how developmental pathways shape postnatal cartilage and joint health.

Histology & spatial biology

Quantitative tissue phenotyping, molecular localization, and spatial readouts to connect signaling to structure.

Transcriptomics

Bulk and single-cell/-nucleus strategies to map pathway states across time, space, and disease.
